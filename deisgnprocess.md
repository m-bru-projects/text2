**Step 1: Write the "World"**

Before designing the actual game, document the history of your world. Determine the inciting incidents, political shifts, or ancient tragedies that shaped the current state of affairs.

The Goal: Create a massive foundational document that only you will see. You need to know the history perfectly so you can casually reference it later without needing to explain it.

Document this in the game folder, without literally implementing this in the game.

**Step 2: Choose Your "Iceberg" Setting**

Limit your actual playable area. Do not try to simulate a whole planet. Choose a highly constrained setting—a single quarantine zone, a stranded submarine, or a dying estate.

The Goal: Build a dense micro-setting. Use casual dialogue and environmental details to imply the massive 90% of the world (the rest of the iceberg) that exists off-screen.

**Step 3: Shatter the Exposition**

Take the timeline you wrote in Step 1, break it into tiny pieces, and scatter it across your setting. 

The Goal: Hide the lore in diegetic materials. Let the player piece the world together through torn journal entries, propaganda posters on the walls, hacked emails, and off-hand remarks from NPCs.

**Step 4: Draft the "Examine" Layer (Sensory Detail)**

Write surface-level descriptions for quick navigation, but write deep, atmospheric, multi-sensory text for when players investigate specifically.

The Goal: When a player examines a desk, do not just tell them it is made of wood. Describe the specific, faded insignias carved into it, the smell of rotting paper, and the unsettling angle of the chair. Reward curiosity with atmosphere.

**Step 5: Map a puzzle graph and design diegetic obstacles**

map out how your puzzles connect using a flowchart or dependency graph. Determine which puzzles are linear (A must be solved to reach B) and which are parallel (A, B, and C can be solved in any order to unlock D).

Design puzzles that require the player to understand your world-building to solve them. Avoid arbitrary "find the blue key for the blue door" mechanics. Puzzles should not feel like random brainteasers dropped into a room; they must reflect the culture, faction, or character that built them. Group smaller puzzles into thematic chains. Instead of having a puzzle simply open a single door, have solving a puzzle trigger a macro-change in the game's environment that affects multiple other puzzles.

The Goal: If the player needs to bypass a security system, make the solution rely on them learning the daily schedule of the guards from a discarded shift log, or understanding the religious superstitions of the faction that built the lock.

Document this in the game folder.

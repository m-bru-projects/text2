# The Thaw - Foundational World

## The Long Winter

In 2189, the northern governments stopped calling the disaster a temporary climate inversion and began calling it the Long Winter. The inversion was not simple cooling. It was a cascade: orbital mirrors shattered in the Second Kessler Weather, ash from equatorial firestorms folded into the upper atmosphere, and the warmed oceans began throwing vapor into air that could no longer hold it. Snow fell where rain had been legal scripture. Ports became cliffs. The old coastlines vanished under pressure ridges that moaned for years.

The Republic of Vey was one of the last states to survive as a state. It did so by replacing ordinary civic life with heat rationing, oath registries, and debt-service inheritance. Its slogan, "Warmth Is Witness," began as a mutual aid phrase after the first blackout winter. Within twenty years it had become the language of courts, marriages, prisons, and taxation.

## Veyr Station and the Covenant of Heat

Veyr Station was built under the Skeld Ice Shelf as a polar transfer archive. It stored not only books and state records but biological seedstock, legal identities, pre-Winter music, cultural replicas, and the names of the missing. The station's power came from the Tide Engine, a geothermal and tidal pressure machine designed by Ruvan Kest, an engineer who hated politics and therefore built one of the most political machines in history.

The Covenant of Heat was created after the White Parliament Massacre of 2224, when city delegates tried to seize Veyr Station's reserves and the station militia sealed them outside during a katabatic storm. The official story says the delegates died attempting sabotage. Private letters suggest they were bargaining for refugee children. In either case, the Covenant taught that heat belonged to those who could preserve memory, not those who merely survived.

This doctrine made Veyr Station both sanctuary and jail. Families sent children to become copyists and furnace clerks. Villages traded their dead people's diaries for battery time. Every legal act needed a warmth witness: a bell, a seal, a recorded oath, and a measurable transfer of heat.

## The Bell Calendar

Veyr Station used bells because electronics failed in rime storms and because bells could be heard through layers of ice, steel, and water. The Founding Toll is the most important sequence: four, one, seven, two. Officially it commemorates the four shelters, one engine, seven seed vaults, and two civic vows. Unofficially it is also the casualty count from the first winter council in compressed form: 4,172 people removed from ration rolls after the Covenant was signed.

The sequence appears everywhere: in nursery songs, boiler prayers, dumbwaiter latches, archive shelfmarks, and the hand position of saintly statues. Citizens learned it before letters. Outsiders hear only a ritual. Vey citizens hear a lock.

## The Great Keeping

The Great Keeping was Veyr Station's century-long project to preserve every surviving name. It failed in two ways.

First, the project grew so large that it began deciding which lives were narratively useful. A midwife's notebook might be preserved because it named a mayor's ancestor; a fisherman's full testimony might be pulped because the family could not pay its heat tithe.

Second, the archivists discovered the Soft Ledger: an illegal index of those erased, misfiled, or intentionally traded away. The Soft Ledger proved that the Covenant was not merely harsh. It was fraudulent. Heat had not followed merit, need, age, or memory. It had followed leverage.

## The Salt Choir

The Salt Choir began as a smuggler's network inside the under-ice service canals. Its members sang coded tide calls because microphones iced over but human voices carried through service pipes. The Choir brought medicine into Veyr Station and names out of it. Over time it became half resistance, half church, and half black market, which is three halves because people in Vey learned to survive by arithmetic that did not satisfy priests.

The Choir's central belief is that a name does not belong to the archive that stores it. A name belongs to the living mouth willing to carry it. Their rites use salt, breath, and unfrozen water.

## The Thaw

The game begins during the first sustained warm front in ninety-six years. The ice shelf above Veyr Station is not simply melting. It is moving. Pressure doors have warped, sealed archives are sweating, ancient fungus is blooming in painted saints' eyes, and the Tide Engine is taking in brine through cracks that were once considered mythological.

The immediate crisis: Veyr Station will flood within hours unless the Tide Engine is rebalanced and the signal lantern is raised to summon evacuation barges from the Free Melt.

The human history: every person in the station has inherited a moral debt from the Covenant of Heat. Some defended it, some suffered under it, some sold pieces of themselves to survive it.

The wider world: outside the ice shelf, new polities are forming along open water. The Free Melt is neither utopia nor raider fleet. It is hungry, improvisational, and suspicious of old archives. If Veyr Station sends only a distress signal, it may be occupied. If it sends the Soft Ledger with the signal, it may become evidence, warning, and confession.

## Principal Characters

Mara Venn is a junior archivist whose mother vanished from the official rolls. She has been trained to preserve the Covenant's documents and to mistrust the Covenant's mercy.

Ione Glass is a Salt Choir courier posing as a lamp mechanic. She wants the Soft Ledger transmitted before the station leadership can burn it. She also wants her younger brother's ration conviction erased, and these goals do not always point in the same direction.

Brother Caldus is the bell chaplain. He loves the station with the horror of a parent who knows his child has committed crimes. He believes confession must precede rescue, but he is afraid confession will kill the few people left.

Pavel Orra is the last tide engineer awake in the lower station. He is practical, exhausted, and furious that everyone romanticizes a machine that mostly wants grease, numbers, and honest pressure readings.

Governor Edrik Vale is dead, but his recorded legal persona still controls parts of the Reliquary. It speaks in polished civic phrases and cannot understand that the moral climate has changed.
